My Dad, your Great-Grandfather, was a ship’s plumber and coppersmith, and when he became Foreman, he was the youngest on the Clyde. This was at the time when shipping on the Clyde was the pride of Glasgow. Then they called him 'the hat', he wore a bowler, different from the men’s bunnets, and maybe it protected him, too, when rivets were dropped by accident from a height.

After Aunt Polly died, we moved to Scotstoun – Renfrew was a bit far for Dad to travel all the time. He had to cross by the Renfrew ferry, rotten in bad weather, it was the only paying ferry on the Clyde, operated by chains, and cost a half penny to cross each way.

All this time, Dad had been in Barkley Curle’s West Yard, so we got one of their houses a semi-detached villa in a small street, and I went to school in Victoria Drive Secondary. It was set in a field with pastures all around, but now it’s all houses and the Safeway's.

(Och, Leah, sometimes I hardly recognize my Glasgow now, with all the flats and motorways). When I was walking to school Uncle David would pass driving the lorry, the horses were almost gone by then, I think I was about eight. He’d blow the horn, shout something cheeky, and call in at Mum’s for cuppa and a cake.

Later, Dad was offered a job by Henry Main of the Caledon Ship-building Yard, in Dundee, so in 1934 we moved to Bonnie Dundee to 8 Greendykes Road. I was fifteen. It was just about three miles from Broughty Ferry, and the beach so that became our Sunday walk, after church and Sunday-school. We had great pleasure when we got visitors from Glasgow for the day (six shillings return).
