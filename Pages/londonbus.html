When I got on that bus, the windows were steamed up, the bodies were packed tight. There were four Polish soldiers, all in their uniforms, facing each other, two and two, and one of them stood up to let me sit – I was going to my shift watching for fighter planes and bombers. The others, oh they, flirted, we expected that. It was the war! London was full of interesting strangers!

And the next day, wasn’t there a different Polish gentleman waiting for me after work? Your Grandpa’s Batman, enquiring if I would care to join Captain Minor for dinner. Your Grandpa was the soldier who had stood and given me his seat, I’d hardly even noticed his face or his name, but he had notice mine! Of course I said no. But he sent his Batman back every day that week, in his shiny boots. After the first time he never said anything, just waited patiently. The other girls thought it was terribly romantic. In the end I said yes, of course, partly out of weariness, and just to see what would happen. It was the war, you know, it seemed as if anything was possible.
