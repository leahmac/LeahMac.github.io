Walerian Antoni Minor, uniformed, chivalrous, on a crowded London bus in 1941 (It was, green, I think. In the war. You know, the kind that used to have conductors.), surrounded by a babble of language he didn’t speak or understand. Incomprehensibly romantic, he was an officer of the Polish Lancers, the gentlemen who charged Nazi panzer tanks with lances and horses at the Polish borders in September 1939. When all was lost he fled south to the village, buried his most precious belongings in the garden (we dug them up again, in 1967, his good binoculars, some of his mother's jewellery), and he escaped south with friends, ski-ing over the Tatra mountains to Hungary.

In Czechoslovakia he was jailed and escaped again and alas we have lost the stories of how he came, eventually to Paris (broke his kneecap, lost an eardrum), a swarm of Poles who miraculously joined the Allies. And then after France was lost another dash across the water to England, and muddy camps in muddy fields and soul-scouring reports coming from Poland about death and genocide. Hard to fathom the anxiety and pain of the man, away from family, country, language.
