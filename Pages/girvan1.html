Girvan. Invergarvane, inbhir gearr abhainn – on the banks of the Girvan river (blackest river I’ve ever seen, black with years of coal dust). Robert the Bruce held court here many years ago, before he was King, and all the other Lords of Carrick. Rabbie Burns dallied here, too, in his licentious youth. Market town, fishing village, holiday destination, haven for smugglers and other heroic wanderers who captured my imagination from time to time…in my lifetime it lapsed into late twentieth century decline. Place of my magic childhood summers, I could see, can still see, the boarded up shop windows on Dalrymple Street; rubbish drifting in the gutters; used condoms in the bathing hut on the promenade; dirty sand and a grubby almost apologetic children’s fun-fair at the pier. (Tell me again about the time I screamed on the walzer Gran?)

Just below the surface are the lives and stories of all the generations before, somehow still living. My Gran showed me with stories how to screw up my eyes the just right way, to see beneath the signs of abandonment to other Girvan times. My mother never learned this art, and fidgeted for a more cosmopolitan world. But me, I could smell the cod and mackerel at the harbour, see the hunched fish-wives with their lightning fast knives, snicker-snack, slicing cleaning, tossing fish into the creels. At the harbour – candy floss, pokey hats of ice cream – we’d sometimes meet Alan McCreth, an old fisherman, who always called my Gran ‘wee Jeannie’. He wore a flat cap and had a hypnotizing curved gap in his front teeth from decades of smoking the same old black pipe that always hung from his mouth. I think he may still be there, pacing the cobbles and watching for the boats coming in.

The Follies – this is where the dances were, Leah, and oh we’d rush down on summer Saturday evenings and buy penny lemonade and giggle about which of the boys was most handsome.
