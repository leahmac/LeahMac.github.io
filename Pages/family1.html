In childhood I was surrounded by a large family, a host of characters, long dead; her magic surrounded our nuclearity with a crowd. “(God bless) Nan, Polly, Willy, Betty, Jean, Ina and David”, prayer mantra of Aunts and Uncles, don’t miss any. Visions of Christmas dinner for forty, picnics with gramophones and tartan rugs, and rabble clowning around a piano. And gossip and gossip over tea in Partick – a Glasgow neighbourhood of families scrambling up the social ladder. (“Javad says Partick reminds him of Iran” says my Aunt of her young Iranian lover, this week). Nan, Great-Grandmother, stares severely from silver-framed black-and-whites, dead at fifty from ‘women’s trouble’, mysteriously immune to the accretions and character embellishment that have technicolourized the others over time. Who was she? Now I examine the solid outlines of her body and see my own.

David, the young one, the bad boy…married down (he didn’t grow up speaking like that, you know, it was just to annoy us), chopped off his fingers with a table saw. Horrors! We sat entranced by the fleshy nubs, unable to look away. Delighted in causing trouble, that one. In sepia tones, I see him nonetheless in the flesh, frowning in his Sunday kilt and polished shoes, forced into Scots respectability. Aunt Polly who went to England and died, that can happen, you know, but of course it was really in childbirth. Willy always on horseback in stories, though it was his Canadian son, Alistair, who was a jockey, won prizes.
