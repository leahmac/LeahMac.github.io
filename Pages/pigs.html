Milky tea and chocolates and old woollen shawls wrapped around our shoulders smelling of damp, warm bodies and comfort. Big flowery wallpaper (look, look, there, can’t you see the lady’s face in it? You have to kind of half-close your eyes…) and the faint ever-present moist chill of Glasgow and the Clyde, banished only as far as the corners by erratic central heating. Grandpa hovers solicitously in the background.

Once up on a time (wheesht now and listen, this is a different one), there were three little pigs, growing up round and happy on a farm in Ayrshire. One day, it was time for them to make their way in the world, and their mother sent them off with the farmer to market to sell, even though she had tears in her eyes and a fear of bacon factories. The little pigs bumped along together for a while, and decided in conversation that really, they’d much rather just set out on their own, and didn’t need the farmer’s help at all. But do you think they could catch his attention? Not at all. And there were no windows in that lorry, and when they rattled the door from the inside, it wouldn’t budge: it was locked with a great big padlock. The three little pigs were quite worried. Suddenly, they felt as though they were hurtling down a mountainside, and were thrown all in a heap to the back of the lorry, thumpety thump, help, and all to the front again. And oh! suddenly at the bottom of the slope they were all three bounced in the air by a huge bump (a sleeping policeman probably), and when they landed with a smack, and blinked, they were amazed to discover themselves sitting on the cobbles. They’d bounced right through the rusty bottom of the lorry!

Well! They brushed the rust out of their hair and looked about, and realized they’d come flying down the Avenue into sunny Girvan (fancy that). For a while they huddled together under the bridge over the burn, and worried. What to do? "Surely the farmer will notice we’re missing?" said one. "Keep a lookout for the lorry!" said another in a quavery voice. "He’ll probably send his wolf to look for us", said the third, almost dead with fright. I’ve seen that wolf, coorying up to the farmer inside his house. We’d better build ourselves houses, to keep ourselves safe.

The first little pig looked about and decided to build his house out of sweetie wrappers and crisp packets. "Nice and waterproof!" he thought. And there were plenty, just lying around. All he had to do was walk along the gutters and pick up the rubbish that the bad boys and girls had thrown away. The second little pig decided to build his house out of coke cans. "Those are stronger than sweetie wrappers!", he thought, "and a lovely red colour! I’ll stack them together into walls". But the third little pig, he was determined that the wolf would never get into his house. He built his house from cement that he borrowed from a cement-mixer at the building site next to the harbour, and to make his house even stronger he fetched some rocks from the Byne Hill, so the walls of his house were sharp and bumpy – just the thing to keep wolves away.

One day, almost when they thought they were safe, and were getting a wee bit lazy, that wily wolf finally found the little pigs' houses. He came to the first little pig in his house of sweetie papers and crisp packets, and shouted "little pig, little pig, let me in!". "Not by the hair of my chinny chin chin!", said the little pig. "Well I'll huff and I'll puff and I'll blow your house in!" said that big bad wolf...and he did blow the house in, the crisp packets lifted up into the air like a parachute, and the first little pig ran squealing to the house of his second brother. The wolf came to the second house built of coke cans then, and begged "little pigs, little pigs, let me in!". "Not by the hair of our chinny chin chins!", said the little pigs. "Well I'll huff and I'll puff and I'll blow your house in!" said that big bad wolf...and he did blow the house in, and the coke cans were flattened and bounced all over the streets, and the two little pigs ran squealing to the house of their third brother. Finally, the wolf came to the cement house, and moaned "little pig, little pig, let me in!". "Not by the hair of our chinny chin chins!", said the little pigs, all together. "Well I'll huff and I'll puff and I'll blow your house in!" said that big bad wolf. And he huffed and puffed but he could not blow down that cement house.

Now that wolf was a sneaky old wolf and he climbed up on the roof to look for a way into the cement house. The little pigs saw the wolf climb up on the roof and lit a roaring fire in the fireplace and put a great big pot of water on to boil. When the wolf finally found the hole in the chimney he crawled down and fell KERSPLASH right into that pot of water. That was the end of their troubles with the big bad wolf, and next time we walk up the Byne, I'll show you where they are living still.
