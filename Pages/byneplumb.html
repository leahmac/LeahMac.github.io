At the Byne Hill, she recites familiar landmarks from her memory – look! you can see all the way to Turnberry from here, oh, the picnics we would have on the beach! in the summer the hedgerows were full of flowers – and conjures lovers in lover’s lane, and old-fashioned cone-shaped canvas tents in the campsite at the foot.

The art of remembering, I learned, is to read like braille the map that the land and the streets have graven in your mind, and to tell the stories that the ridges and peaks summon.


We walked the back streets, she and me, pacing over a 1970s car park of tar macadam (he was a Scotsman, you know) and concrete. Plum Square, she’d say, this is where Granny lived, where we lived, and she could conjure the cottages next to the bowling green with a gesture and a nod…and there’s where we took that picture of Uncle David in his kilt, and there’s where Grandpa tied up his cart-horses when he came in for his dinner. In books, I read with surprise that the street was Plumb Square, named after a Plumb, a deep well. In my little girl mind it was an orchard, sugar plums, place of fairies.
