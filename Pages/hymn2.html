Now, after her death, I discover that there were secrets kept, stories untold. My mother’s birth date: March, 1944; Gran's wedding day: June, 1944. I wonder about ‘homes for unwed mothers’ and Grandpa stationed with his troops in a muddy field. My Grandmother’s sister, in tears, says “she swore me to secrecy, she didn’t want your mother to think badly of her, I wasn’t allowed to tell."

At her funeral, we sang her choice (a list of wishes left in her bureau) – "The King of Love My Shepherd Is" – and I laughed out loud through tears at verse three.


In her Sunday School prize bible, I find a poem about death and remembrance, and the ribbon marker at the luscious Song of Solomon.

The King of love my shepherd is
whose goodness faileth never;
I nothing lack if I am his,
and he is mine for ever.

Where streams of living water flow,
my ransomed soul he leadeth,
and where the verdant pastures grow,
with food celestial feedeth.

Perverse and foolish oft I strayed,
but yet in love He sought me,
and on his shoulder gently laid,
and home, rejoicing, brought me.

In death's dark vale I fear no ill
with thee, dear Lord, beside me;
thy rod and staff my comfort still,
thy cross before to guide me.

Thou spread'st a table in my sight;
thy unction grace bestoweth;
and O what transport of delight
from thy pure chalice floweth!

And so through all the length of days
thy goodness faileth never:
Good Shepherd, may I sing thy praise
within thy house for ever.
